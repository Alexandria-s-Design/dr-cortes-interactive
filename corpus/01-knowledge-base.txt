Professional Chronology of Dr. Carlos E. Cortés
Authoritative Version — February 19, 2026

=== 1950s: The Road to Riverside ===

1955-1956: Editor, Blue and Gold (yearbook), University of California, Berkeley. Served as editor of the university's yearbook, launching his career in communications.

1955: Chair, Student Publications Board, University of California, Berkeley. Oversaw campus publications and editorial operations as an undergraduate leader.

1956: B.A., Communications and Public Policy (Phi Beta Kappa), University of California, Berkeley. Graduated with highest academic honors.

1957: M.S., Journalism, Columbia Graduate School of Journalism. Ivy League training grounded his lifelong commitment to clear, accessible communication.

1957: Press Assistant, American Shakespeare Festival, Stratford, Connecticut. Applied journalism training to arts communications.

1957-1959: Public Information Specialist, Fort Gordon, Georgia. Military service in institutional communications and public affairs.

1959-1961: Editor, Phoenix Sunpapers, Phoenix, Arizona. Professional journalism experience preceded his pivot to academic life.

=== 1960s: Becoming a Historian ===

1962: Bachelor of Foreign Trade, American Institute for Foreign Trade, Glendale, Arizona. Growing interest in international affairs and Latin America.

1965: M.A., Portuguese, University of New Mexico. Language training for doctoral research on Brazilian politics.

1966-1967: Doctoral dissertation research in Brazil with a Ford Foundation Foreign Area Fellowship. Field research on regional politics in Rio Grande do Sul.

1968: Began career as a history professor at the University of California, Riverside (UCR). Launched a 57-year association with UCR.

1969: Ph.D., History, University of New Mexico. Dissertation on regional politics in southern Brazil.

1969: Member of the committee that designed and founded the Mexican American Studies Program (later renamed Chicano Studies) at UCR.

=== 1970s: Lurching into K-12 Education ===

1970: Taught UCR's first Chicano History course. Pioneered Chicano Studies as an academic discipline at UC Riverside.

1970: Became chair of UCR's Latin American Studies program, serving until 1972.

1971: Served on the Ethnic Content Textbook Task Force of the California State Department of Education. First state-level effort to evaluate textbooks for ethnic representation.

1972: Became chair of UCR's Mexican American Studies Program (later renamed Chicano Studies), serving until 1979.

1972: Co-produced and co-authored the historical documentary film "Northwest from Tumacácori."

1973: "Teaching the Chicano Experience" in James Banks's Teaching Ethnic Studies: Concepts and Strategies. When Banks's book became widely adopted for teacher training, Cortés was launched onto the national K-12 multicultural education speaking circuit.

1974: Published Gaúcho Politics in Brazil: The Politics of Rio Grande do Sul, 1930-1964. Received the Hubert Herring Memorial Award; published in Brazil in 2007 as Política Gaúcha.

1974: Edited The Mexican American (21-volume reprint series) for Arno Press, followed by The Chicano Heritage (55 volumes, 1976) and Hispanics in the United States (30 volumes, 1980).

1975: Co-organized the first California statewide bilingual education conference at UCR, which led to the formation of the California Association for Bilingual Education (CABE).

1976: UCR Distinguished Teaching Award.

1976: Co-edited Three Perspectives on Ethnicity: Blacks, Chicanos, and Native Americans.

1979: "The Societal Curriculum and the School Curriculum: Allies or Antagonists?" Introduced the influential concept of the "societal curriculum."

=== 1980s: The All-Purpose Multiculturalist ===

1980: Distinguished California Humanist Award, California Council for the Humanities.

1980: Wrote essay "Mexicans" for the Harvard Encyclopedia of American Ethnic Groups.

1982: Began teaching an annual course, History of the Mass Media, at UCR.

1982: Became UCR History Department chair (1982-1986).

1982: Became periodic guest presenter on the PBS national series "Why in the World?" (three appearances through 1984).

1983: Served as the Bildner Fellow of the Association of American Schools in South America.

1983: Wrote the PBS documentary "Latinos: A Growing Voice in U.S. Politics."

1985: Began as a columnist for Media & Values: A Quarterly Review of Media Issues and Trends (until 1990).

1986: Helped establish the Rupert and Jeannette Costo Chair in American Indian History and the Costo Library at UCR.

1986: Co-authored Beyond Language: Social and Cultural Factors in Schooling Language Minority Students.

1986: Spent four weeks in Japan as a Japan Foundation Fellow.

1988: Edited "Images and Realities of Four World Regions."

=== 1990s: Everybody's Adjunct ===

1990: Became a faculty member of the Harvard Summer Institutes for Higher Education.

1991: Consultant for the Japanese National Chamber of Commerce.

1992: Began service on the advisory committee for "Talking with TJ," a Hallmark Foundation youth conflict resolution media series.

1992: Gave lecture "The Man Masks of Multicultural Education" at UCLA.

1992: UCR Faculty Public Service Award.

1993: Appointed to the National Panel of the AAC project, American Commitments: Diversity, Democracy, and Liberal Learning.

1994: Featured presenter for the Video Journal of Education's "Diversity in the Classroom."

1994: Took early retirement from the University of California.

1995: Became a faculty member of the Summer Institute for Intercultural Communication.

1995: Lecture tour of Australian universities.

1999: Became a faculty member of the Federal Executive Institute.

1999: Founding coordinator of the Riverside, California, Mayor's Multicultural Forum.

=== 2000s: Curtain Going Up ===

2000: Faculty member of the Civicus Living-Learning Program at the University of Maryland, College Park.

2000: Published The Children Are Watching: How the Media Teach about Diversity. Landmark analysis of media's pedagogical function.

2000: Became consultant (later Creative/Cultural Advisor) for Nickelodeon's Dora the Explorer (also Go, Diego, Go!, Dora and Friends: Into the City, and Santiago of the Seas). Championed making Dora "pan-Latino."

2001: Outstanding Contribution to Higher Education Award, NASPA.

2001: Facilitated and wrote the Riverside, California, Inclusive Community Statement.

2002: Published The Making — and Remaking — of a Multiculturalist. Autobiographical educational memoir.

2003: First performance of one-person autobiographical play "A Conversation with Alana: One Boy's Multicultural Rite of Passage." Performed approximately 160 times nationwide.

2004: UCR Emeritus Faculty Award.

2005: Co-authored Houghton Mifflin's K-6 Social Studies textbook series and McDougal Littell World History textbook series.

2005: Wrote, narrated, and co-produced "After the Rain: Tomás Rivera — The Legacy and Life."

2007: Honorary Doctorate, College of Wooster.

2009: NAACP Image Award for Nickelodeon advisory work.

=== 2010s: Winding Down ===

2010: Honorary Doctorate, DePaul University (Chicago).

2011: Co-authored the book and lyrics and co-produced the musical "We Are Not Alone: Tomás Rivera, a Musical Narrative" (with Juan Felipe Rivera, music by Bruno Louchouarn).

2012: Published Rose Hill: An Intermarriage before Its Time. Memoir about growing up in an interracial, interfaith family in Kansas City.

2013: Editor of the four-volume Multicultural America Encyclopedia (SAGE Publications, 900+ entries).

2014: Member of the Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences project for Pacific Standard Time: LA/LA.

2016: City of Riverside established the Carlos E. Cortés Diversity and Inclusion Award.

2016: Named Edward A. Dickson Emeritus Professor, UCR.

2016: Published Fourth Quarter: Reflections of a Cranky Old Man (poetry). Received Honorable Mention, International Latino Book Awards.

2018: Inaugural fellow of the UC National Center for Free Speech and Civic Engagement.

2019: Columnist for the American Diversity Report with two series: "Diversity and Speech" and "Renewing Diversity."

2019: Played the "Big Bad Wolf" in a Los Angeles reading of "The Shit Show" by Leelee Jackson.

2019: Initial draft of "Ethnic Studies Graduation Requirement: Suggested Basic Curriculum Principles" for California Board of Education. Principles were adapted into the Model Curriculum adopted March 2021.

=== 2020s: Zombie Time ===

2020: Wrote the Riverside, California, Anti-Racism Vision Statement (adopted 6-1 by City Council, October 20, 2020).

2020: Appointed co-director of the Health Equity, Social Justice, and Anti-Racism (HESJAR) curricular thread of the UCR School of Medicine (with Dr. Adwoa Osei).

2020: Cultural Advisor for Nickelodeon's Santiago of the Seas.

2021: Constantine Panunzio Distinguished Emeriti Award, University of California (first faculty member from UCR to receive this honor).

2021: Consulting Humanist, Cheech Marin Center for Chicano Art and Culture. Conducts filmed "Conversations at The Cheech."

2022: A Conversation with Alana: One Boy's Multicultural Rite of Passage (published as a book).

2022: Appointed to the Teachers Pay Teachers Content Moderation Task Force.

2024: Cultural Consultant, "Puss in Boots: The Last Wish" (DreamWorks Animation).

2024: "Renewing Multicultural Education: An Ancient Mariner's Manifesto" published in Multicultural Perspectives.

2025: Published Scouts' Honor (debut novel, mystery set at Boy Scout Camp Matulia).

2025: Contributing author, Creating the Intercultural Field: Legacies from the Pioneers.

2026: Multilingual Educator Hall of Fame, National Association for Bilingual Education (NABE).
